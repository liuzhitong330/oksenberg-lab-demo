CNS Resilience Across Brain Regions: Why Brainstem and Cerebellar MS Lesions Drive the Most Disability

Cathy Liu · cathyliu014@gmail.com · personal exploratory project, July 2026
multiple sclerosis CNS resilience DYSF-ZNF638 myelin BDNF severity genetics Allen Brain Atlas Python

The Oksenberg lab has spent decades building the genetic and functional genomic framework for understanding multiple sclerosis — from the HLA associations that explain over 10% of MS heritability, to the 200+ susceptibility loci identified through GWAS, to the current push toward understanding why genetic risk converts to different levels of disability in different people.

The 2023 Nature paper from the IMSGC (with Oksenberg as a key contributor) reframed a fundamental question: while immune genetics explains who gets MS, it does not explain how fast the disease progresses. The identification of DYSF-ZNF638 as the first MS severity locus — with heritability enriched in CNS rather than immune tissue — points to CNS resilience as the key. I wanted to ask the next question from the expression side: do brain regions that drive the most disability in MS also have the lowest neuroprotective reserve relative to their myelin load?

What this is. Personal exploratory analysis, not novel research. Gene expression data from the Allen Mouse Brain Atlas ISH experiments, July 2026. Genes: Nefl (exp. 73512198), Plp1 (exp. 75496529), Mag (exp. 69736418), Gfap (exp. 100098224), Bdnf (exp. 77003265). Brain structure IDs: Ctx=453, BS=771, Cereb=512, Thal=549, Hipp=382, CP=247, CC=484. Figure 2 is a schematic based on IMSGC (2023) Nature — not a re-analysis of their data.

Brainstem and cerebellum: highest myelin load, lowest neuroprotective reserve

Cerebellum has a Plp1/Bdnf ratio of 41× — 8× higher than hippocampus (4.8×) and 24× higher than corpus callosum (1.7×). Plp1 encodes proteolipid protein 1, the most abundant CNS myelin protein, and its expression density is a proxy for myelin content and oligodendrocyte density. Bdnf supports oligodendrocyte survival, promotes remyelination, and protects axons from inflammatory damage. A region with high Plp1 but near-zero Bdnf (cerebellum: 0.0017, brainstem: 0.0040) has substantial myelin to lose but minimal molecular capacity for neuroprotective repair — exactly the profile you would predict for a region where lesions cause disproportionate disability and where recovery after relapses is poorest.
Corpus callosum has the highest Bdnf expression (0.032) of any region examined — and is the primary MS lesion site. This seems counterintuitive, but it illustrates the distinction the Oksenberg lab's 2023 paper makes explicit: lesion location is driven by immune genetics (HLA, immune cell trafficking), while disability accumulation is driven by CNS resilience. The corpus callosum is attacked preferentially by immune mechanisms, but its high Bdnf may partially explain why periventricular lesions, despite being frequent and visible on MRI, often convert to disability more slowly than a single brainstem or cerebellar lesion of equivalent volume.
The DYSF-ZNF638 severity locus may act by modifying how well individual oligodendrocytes and axons survive and repair after immune attack. Dysferlin (DYSF) is a membrane repair protein. In the CNS, its role in oligodendrocyte and axonal membrane integrity after inflammatory damage would have the largest effect in regions with the least compensatory Bdnf-driven repair — precisely brainstem and cerebellum. This predicts that the DYSF risk allele should have larger functional effects in cerebellar-progressive MS subtypes, a testable hypothesis the lab's MS-EPIC cohort data could address.

Two genetic programs: susceptibility vs. severity

Connection to the lab's work

  • CNS resilience and the severity locus: The DYSF-ZNF638 finding opens the question of which molecular pathway links this locus to regional differences in repair capacity. I'd be interested to contribute to analyses asking whether Dysf expression in oligodendrocytes or astrocytes correlates with remyelination efficiency across lesion types — particularly in the lab's existing multi-omics or methylation datasets.
  • Biomarkers and disease progression: The lab's work on neurofilament light (NfL) as a progression biomarker, and the 2024 autoantibody signature finding (years before symptom onset), suggests a growing focus on the preclinical and early-progressive phase of MS. I'd be glad to contribute computational analysis for longitudinal biomarker work connecting genetic severity stratification to NfL trajectories.
  • On the wet lab side, I have experience with molecular biology techniques including Western blot, ELISA, cell culture, flow cytometry, and fluorescence microscopy. I'm comfortable contributing to both computational and bench aspects of the work.
